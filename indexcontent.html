Week 12

Project Status

The main purpose of this week is to find and test the optimal point the camera should be pointed to. So some of requirements to allow the license plate to read is to have
1. High accuracy of the plate and correct of the plate
2. Correct distance from camera
3. Full vision of plate I am aware that my testing sample is small and there some consistency on the photo due to field of vision need to be adjusted. The testing method is taking tests of the plate is vision and changing the distance from the image to the camera with increments of 15 cm


This is the original license plate that being used

This is the result of 15 cm

This is the result of 30 cm

This is the result of 45 cm

This is the result of 60 cm

This is the result of 75 cm

So the observation is the plate is giving the correct plate number from 15 cm to 60cm. On the 75cm distance its getting the wrong distance.

Another way to find the optimal distance is making sure the first result has a very high confidence. so this occurs around 15 cm to 45 cm with around 90% confidence of what the license plate is trying to recognize.

The final thing to filter out to figure out the best distance is to make sure the 2nd confidence level should be a big delta compared to the first confidence level plate. This will ensure that it is less likely that the 2nd wrong license plate to be recognized as first guess plate. Looking into the data around 30 cm and 45 cm ensure around 8% or 10% difference. Thus around 30 cm and between 45 will become the best result.

So with all considerations it seems the image at 45 cm is deemed to be best distance to take photo of the plate numbers

    Week 0

    • Solder together a sensor hat (parts provided from J206) for a Raspberry Pi 3 (that you purchase). This Broadcom development platform was chosen for a number of reasons. Let us start with platform requirements, it should be able to be used to create a small programmable device capable of connecting to enterprise Wi-Fi, bluetooth, and I2C. The NXP HCS12 can have a network stack loaded on it, but then not be able to do much more and thus the device would require multiple HCS12s for this purpose causing an increase in cost. Likewise with Atmel based Arduinos, connecting them to enterprise Wi-Fi is not straighforward. TI's beaglebone shows promise as an alternative however you would need to redesign the sensor hat in order to be compatible.
    • Kits of sensor hat parts should be available in J206 (hours posted outside the door) to get started before the term starts if you like.
    • Here are some videos on soldering: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BLfXXRfRIzY&list=PLQ32vZrF5U2lFOJTtZDytBWBYVLNp4RYz be sure to get Kelly and Vlad’s input on how to solder safely in J206.
    • I have my Pi 3 in this case http://www.canakit.com/raspberry-pi-black-case.html and we bought USB network adaptors from Tiger Direct.
